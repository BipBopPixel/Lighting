Chapter 01

What is Light?

Light is electromagnetic radiation detectable by our eyes — a tiny slice of a vast spectrum. Understanding this is the foundation of everything else in lighting.

← Ultraviolet 380 nm Visible Light 700 nm Infrared →

Speed of Light

Light travels at 299,792 km/s — the universal speed limit. It reaches Earth from the Sun in 8 minutes and circles the globe 7.5 times per second.

Wavelength = Color

450nm = blue, 550nm = green, 650nm = red. The human eye can distinguish roughly 10 million distinct colors across this narrow visible band.

Rods & Cones

Your retina has 120M rod cells for low-light vision, and 6M cone cells for color. Peak cone sensitivity is ~555nm — the green-yellow that LEDs are optimized around.

Heat vs. Light

Incandescent bulbs convert ~90% of energy to heat, only 10% to light. LEDs flip this — over 90% becomes light. That efficiency gap is what drove the entire LED revolution.

Chapter 02

The History of Lighting

From fire to photons — 400,000 years of humanity pushing back the dark. Every era changed how we live, work, and see the world.

~400,000 BC

Fire — The First Light

Controlled fire gave early humans warmth and the ability to see after dark. Cave paintings at Lascaux were made by firelight. Everything that followed is a refinement of the same idea: generate heat to produce light.

~3,000 BC

Oil Lamps & Candles

Egyptians and Romans used tallow wicks for portable light. Beeswax candles were a luxury — cleaner burning and fragrant. For millennia, this was the ceiling of artificial lighting technology.

1792 – 1810s

Gas Lighting — Cities Wake Up

William Murdoch lit his home with coal gas in 1792. By the 1810s London's streets were gas-lit. For the first time, cities could function after dark — shops stayed open, crime fell, social life extended well beyond sunset.

1792
1879
1879

Edison's Incandescent Bulb

Edison didn't invent electric light — he invented a practical, mass-producible version. His carbon filament lasted 1,200 hours and he built Manhattan's first power grid to go with it. Within decades, electric light replaced gas globally.

1938

Fluorescent — Efficiency Arrives

GE's first commercial fluorescent tube produced 3–4× more light per watt. Offices and factories adopted them fast. But they brought problems: mercury, flicker, hum, and color rendering that made everything look slightly wrong.

1938
1993
1962 → 1993

The LED Revolution

Holonyak's 1962 red LED was just an indicator for 30 years. Then Nakamura's 1993 bright blue LED — Nobel Prize Physics 2014 — unlocked white light: blue chip + yellow phosphor = white. Every LED product in the industry today traces back to that single discovery.

2012

Philips Hue — Smart Lighting Born

Philips launched the world's first mainstream connected lighting system: 16 million colors, app control, scheduling, and music sync. Lighting became software. A new product category was created, and the market has never looked back.

2012
Now
2020s — Now

Light as a Platform

LEDs are now standard. The frontier is AI, software, and biology: circadian-tuned light, daylight harvesting, UV-C air disinfection, LiFi data networks, and circular economy design. The industry's best work is still ahead.

Chapter 04

Types of Light Sources

From Edison's filament to smart connected LEDs — how each technology works, its tradeoffs, and where it still fits today.

Inc.

Incandescent

A tungsten filament is heated until it glows. The original electric light — beautiful warm color (CRI ~100) but 90% of energy is wasted as heat. Phased out by EU, US, and most markets due to efficiency regulations.

CRI ~100~15 lm/W~1,000 hr life2700K
Hal.

Halogen

An improved incandescent — halogen gas recycles tungsten back onto the filament, making it brighter and longer-lived. Still used in car headlights and professional studio photography where perfect CRI is non-negotiable.

CRI ~100~25 lm/W2–5k hoursVery hot
CFL

Fluorescent / CFL

Electric current excites mercury vapour to emit UV light. Phosphor coating converts UV to visible light. 4× more efficient than incandescent — but contains mercury, produces flicker, hum, and poor color rendering. CFLs bridged the gap before LEDs took over.

CRI 60–8560–100 lm/W8–15k hoursHas mercury
LED

LED — Light Emitting Diode

A semiconductor emits light when current flows through a p-n junction. No filament, no mercury, minimal heat. The dominant technology today and the foundation of the modern lighting industry. Tunable from 1800K to 6500K, dimmable, connectable, and extraordinarily long-lasting.

CRI up to 98100–200 lm/W25–50k hoursNo mercury
OLED

OLED — Organic LED

Thin organic compounds emit light across an entire flat panel — not a single point. No glare, no shadows, no hot spots. Ultra-diffuse illumination you can see on phone and TV screens. Now emerging in architectural lighting as costs fall.

Zero glareThin & flexibleHigh costEmerging tech
Smart

Smart / Connected Lighting

LED + wireless connectivity + software. Lights that respond to occupancy, daylight levels, time of day, or manual control via app or voice. Tunable white shifts CCT through the day to match circadian needs. Philips Hue is the world's best-selling connected lighting ecosystem.

Tunable CCTApp + VoiceEnergy savingNeeds network

Chapter 05

How Light Affects Us

Light is the most powerful biological time-cue for the human body. It shapes mood, productivity, sleep, and health — often completely below our awareness.

Color Temperature Through the Day

1800K2700K4000K5500K6500K
2200K

Evening (2200K)

Warm amber mimics sunset. Reduces melatonin suppression and signals sleep preparation to your brain.

3000K

Morning (3000K)

Gentle warm light eases you awake. Standard for hotel breakfasts, cafés, and wake-up light therapy devices.

4000K

Work Hours (4000K)

Neutral white maximises alertness and concentration. The standard across offices, schools, and healthcare facilities.

5000K+

Peak Focus (5000K+)

Cool daylight-equivalent boosts serotonin and suppresses melatonin. Used in windowless labs and precision workspaces.

Circadian Rhythm

Your body clock responds to blue-rich light by suppressing melatonin. Misalignment — from phones, wrong office lighting, or shift work — causes chronic sleep disruption and health consequences.

Productivity

Research shows 4000–5000K lighting increases alertness, reduces errors, and improves reaction time. Inadequate workplace lighting is a leading cause of fatigue — something the industry's connected systems are increasingly designed to solve.

Mood & Emotion

Dim warm light creates intimacy and relaxation. Bright cool light energises and sharpens focus. Seasonal Affective Disorder (SAD) is directly linked to insufficient winter light — treatable with high-lux bright light therapy.

Retail Psychology

Warm light (2700–3000K) with high CRI and R9 makes food look fresh and skin appear healthy. Light direction shapes texture perception. Lighting is retail's most powerful and frequently underestimated tool.

Healthcare

Human-centric lighting in hospitals accelerates patient recovery, reduces medication errors, and stabilises nurses' sleep on shift rotations. Light therapy is clinically proven for depression and circadian disorders.

Learning

Dynamic classroom lighting — brighter and cooler during tests, warmer during creative work — measurably improves focus, reading speed, and information retention in students across all ages.

Glossary
A
ACL
Asymmetric Cross-Lighting
A technique using two light sources positioned at equal angles from opposite sides to reveal texture and three-dimensionality in a surface. Common in façade and retail display lighting.
AFC
Automatic Fault Compensation
A driver feature that maintains output when individual LEDs in a string fail, preventing full circuit failure.
B
BMS
Building Management System
A centralised control network managing HVAC, lighting, access, and security across a building. Lighting systems connect via DALI, KNX, or BACnet gateways.
Example: An Interact-connected BMS dims lighting automatically when occupancy sensors detect an empty floor.
Beam Angle
The angle between the two directions in which the luminous intensity drops to 50% of its peak value. Narrow beams (10–25°) create accent spots; wide beams (60–120°) give ambient fill.
BUG
Backlight / Uplight / Glare rating
An outdoor luminaire classification system (IES TM-15) measuring light trespass, sky glow contribution, and observer glare. Used in dark-sky compliance projects.
C
CCT
Correlated Colour Temperature
The colour appearance of a white light source expressed in Kelvin (K). Lower values (2700K) appear warm/amber; higher values (6500K) appear cool/blue-white. Named after how a blackbody radiator changes colour as it heats.
Example: 3000K is typical for hotel lobbies; 4000K for offices; 5000K+ for surgical suites.
CRI Ra
Colour Rendering Index
A 0–100 measure of how accurately a light source renders object colours compared to a reference source (sunlight/blackbody). Calculated as the average of 8 test colour samples (R1–R8). CRI ≥90 is considered high quality.
DALI
Digital Addressable Lighting Interface
An international standard (IEC 62386) for two-wire digital lighting control. Each device has a unique short address (0–63). Supports 16 groups, 16 scenes, broadcast commands, and feedback queries. Widely used in professional buildings.
Example: A DALI command "Recall Scene 4 to Group 2" dims a meeting room to a presentation preset.
DH
Daylight Harvesting
A control strategy that reduces artificial lighting output when sensors detect sufficient daylight, maintaining a target illuminance while saving energy. Standard in BREEAM and LEED-certified buildings.
E
Efficacy
Luminous efficacy: lumens produced per watt consumed (lm/W). The primary efficiency metric for light sources. Typical values: incandescent ~15, fluorescent ~80, good LED 100–200.
Example: A 30W LED fixture producing 4500 lm has an efficacy of 150 lm/W.
EMC
Electromagnetic Compatibility
A driver and luminaire requirement ensuring the product neither emits excessive electromagnetic interference nor is susceptible to it. Mandatory for CE marking in the EU (EN 55015).
EV / Ecyl
Cylindrical / Vertical Illuminance
Illuminance measured on a vertical or cylindrical surface, used to assess facial visibility and spatial modelling. EN 12464-1 specifies minimum vertical illuminance for security cameras and face recognition tasks.
F
Flicker
Rapid, repetitive variation in light output. Visible flicker (below ~80 Hz) is immediately noticeable. Invisible flicker (80–3000 Hz) still causes headaches and eye strain. Measured by Percent Flicker and Flicker Index (IEEE 1789).
Flux (Luminous)
Total light output of a source in all directions, measured in lumens (lm). The "size" of the light source's total output — the number on bulb packaging.
G–H
Glare
Visual discomfort or disability caused by excessive brightness in the field of view. Quantified by UGR for interior spaces and GR (Glare Rating) for sports/outdoor. Controlled through luminaire shielding, diffusers, and mounting height.
HCL
Human-Centric Lighting
A lighting design philosophy that adjusts intensity and colour temperature throughout the day to support human health, wellbeing, and circadian rhythms. Combines tunable white technology with automated control systems.
Example: An HCL office system automatically shifts from 6500K at 9am to 2700K at 6pm.
I–K
IP
Ingress Protection Rating
A two-digit code (IEC 60529) indicating protection against solid particles (first digit, 0–6) and liquids (second digit, 0–8). IP65 = dust-tight and protected against water jets. Required for outdoor and washdown environments.
ipRGC
Intrinsically Photosensitive Retinal Ganglion Cell
A third type of photoreceptor (distinct from rods and cones) containing melanopsin, with peak sensitivity at ~480nm. Governs circadian rhythm entrainment and melatonin suppression. The biological basis for human-centric lighting design.
IK Rating
Impact protection rating (IEC 62262), separate from IP. IK08 = 5 joules; IK10 = 20 joules. Required for luminaires in vandal-prone or industrial environments.
Kelvin (K)
The unit for colour temperature. Named after Lord Kelvin — based on the colour a theoretical blackbody radiator would emit at that absolute temperature. Has no relationship to how warm or cool a fixture physically feels.
KNX
KNX Building Automation Protocol
An open standard (ISO/IEC 14543) for building automation covering lighting, HVAC, and security over twisted pair, RF, or IP. More complex than DALI but broader in scope — preferred for full building integration.
L
L70 / L80 / L90
Lumen Maintenance
The operating hours at which a light source's output has declined to 70%, 80%, or 90% of its initial value. Defined in IES TM-21 and LM-80. L70 is the standard end-of-life definition for LED products.
LOR
Light Output Ratio
The proportion of a lamp's total flux that actually exits the luminaire housing. A luminaire with LOR 0.85 delivers 85% of its lamp's rated lumens. The rest is absorbed by reflectors, diffusers, and housings.
Lumen (lm)
The SI unit of luminous flux — the total quantity of light emitted by a source per unit time, weighted by the human eye's sensitivity curve (photopic response). Not the same as watts, which measures power consumed.
Lux (lx)
Illuminance: one lumen falling on one square metre. Lux = Lumens / Area. Measured with a luxmeter at the working plane. The value specified in EN 12464-1 standards for different tasks.
Example: A 3500 lm fixture over 10 m² gives approximately 350 lx (before utilisation and maintenance factors).
M
MacAdam
MacAdam Ellipse / SDCM
A region on the CIE chromaticity diagram within which two colours appear identical to the average observer. Measured in Standard Deviations of Colour Matching (SDCM). ≤3 SDCM is imperceptible; >5 SDCM is noticeable side-by-side.
MF
Maintenance Factor
A combined multiplier (typically 0.7–0.85) accounting for lumen depreciation over time, luminaire soiling, and room surface deterioration. Used in lux calculations to ensure illuminance targets are maintained, not just met at day one.
Matter
Matter Protocol
An open, IP-based smart home interoperability standard (CSA, formerly Project CHIP). Allows lighting from different manufacturers to work in the same ecosystem. Supported by Apple HomeKit, Google Home, Amazon Alexa, and SmartThings.
Melanopic EDI
Melanopic Equivalent Daylight Illuminance
The circadian-relevant illuminance metric (CIE S026) replacing raw lux for human-centric lighting design. Weights illuminance by ipRGC sensitivity rather than photopic response. Target: ≥250 melanopic lux at the eye during daytime work.
O–P
OLED
Organic Light Emitting Diode
A thin-film electroluminescent technology emitting light across a flat panel rather than a point source. Produces ultra-diffuse illumination with no visible source. Currently high cost but growing in architectural applications.
PF
Power Factor
The ratio of real power (watts) to apparent power (volt-amperes) drawn by a driver. Good drivers achieve PF >0.9. Low power factor increases current draw on building electrical infrastructure without contributing useful light.
PWM
Pulse Width Modulation
A dimming method that switches the LED on and off rapidly (typically 1–20kHz). At low frequencies, PWM causes visible or physiologically harmful flicker. High-frequency PWM (>3kHz) is generally considered safe per IES TM-30.
P-F / P65
Photobiological Safety
Evaluation of light source hazard to the eye and skin (IEC 62471). Risk groups from 0 (exempt) to 3 (high risk). UV-C disinfection sources require Risk Group 3 handling procedures. All general lighting must declare a risk group.
R
R9
R9 Score
CRI sub-index for saturated red rendering — the most commonly referenced extended test colour. Critical for skin tones, fresh food, and luxury retail. Not included in the base CRI Ra calculation. Desirable values: >50 for retail; >80 for medical and luxury applications.
RI / K
Room Index (Room Cavity Ratio)
A dimensionless number derived from room geometry: K = (L × W) / (H_m × (L + W)). Used to determine the utilisation factor from photometric tables. Higher K values indicate more efficient rooms for direct lighting.
S
SDCM
Standard Deviation of Colour Matching
See MacAdam Ellipse. The practical measure of colour consistency across a batch of LED units. Specified on product datasheets as "≤3 SDCM" or "≤5 SDCM".
Spectral Power Distribution (SPD)
A graph showing the relative intensity of a light source across each wavelength of the visible spectrum. The shape of the SPD determines colour rendering, CCT, and circadian impact. LED SPDs typically show a blue peak (from the chip) and a broad phosphor hump.
T
Ta / Tc / Tj
Ambient / Case / Junction Temperature
Three critical thermal reference points for LED performance. Tj (junction) is the semiconductor temperature — the primary driver of lumen depreciation and lifespan. Kept below 85–125°C through heatsink design. Datasheets specify lumen output at a defined Tc or Ta.
THD
Total Harmonic Distortion
A measure of how much harmonic current a driver injects back into the mains supply. THD >20% can cause issues in buildings with sensitive equipment. Good LED drivers achieve THD <15%.
TM-21
IES TM-21 Lumen Maintenance Projection
An IES technical memorandum providing a standardised method for projecting long-term LED lumen maintenance from short-term LM-80 test data. Limits projections to 6× the test duration to prevent extrapolation errors.
Tunable White
Tunable White
A luminaire technology using two LED channels (typically warm ~2700K and cool ~6500K) that blend to produce any correlated colour temperature in the range. Controlled via DALI DT8, 0–10V dual channel, or wireless protocols.
U–Z
UGR
Unified Glare Rating
A calculated measure (CIE 117) of discomfort glare from interior luminaires, based on luminance, solid angle, and background luminance. Scale 10–30; EN 12464-1 limits: offices ≤19, drawing offices ≤16, corridors ≤25.
Example: UGR<19 requires luminaires with diffusers or microprismatic optics, not bare LEDs.
UV-C
Ultraviolet-C Radiation (200–280 nm)
Short-wavelength UV radiation that inactivates pathogens by disrupting DNA. Used in germicidal disinfection (UVGI) for air and surface treatment. Requires safety interlocks — direct human exposure at high doses is hazardous.
Utilisation Factor (UF)
The proportion of a luminaire's output that reaches the work plane, accounting for room geometry and surface reflectances. Looked up from photometric tables using the Room Index K. Typically 0.4–0.8 in real installations.
Zigbee
Zigbee Protocol
A low-power mesh networking protocol (IEEE 802.15.4) used in smart lighting systems. Operates at 2.4 GHz with up to 250 kbps data rate. Devices form a self-healing mesh — each node extends range. Used in Philips Hue and many commercial systems.

Quick Calculations
Illuminance
Lux from lumens
E = Φ ÷ A
E = illuminance (lx) · Φ = luminous flux (lm) · A = area (m²). Assumes perfect uniform distribution — real installations apply a utilisation factor (UF) and maintenance factor (MF).
Example: 4 × 3500 lm fixtures in a 10 × 8 m room → E = 14,000 ÷ 80 = 175 lx (before UF/MF)
Fixtures needed
N = (E × A) ÷ (Φ × UF × MF)
N = number of fixtures · E = target lux · A = room area (m²) · Φ = lumens per fixture · UF = utilisation factor (0.4–0.8) · MF = maintenance factor (0.7–0.85).
Example: Target 500 lx, 40 m² room, 4000 lm fixture, UF 0.65, MF 0.80 → N = (500 × 40) ÷ (4000 × 0.65 × 0.80) = 20,000 ÷ 2,080 ≈ 10 fixtures
Room Index (K)
Room Index
K = (L × W) ÷ (Hm × (L + W))
L = room length (m) · W = room width (m) · Hm = mounting height above work plane (m). K determines the utilisation factor from photometric tables. Typical range: 0.6 (small/high) to 5 (large/low).
Example: 10 × 8 m room, ceiling 3 m, work plane 0.8 m → Hm = 2.2 m → K = (10 × 8) ÷ (2.2 × (10 + 8)) = 80 ÷ 39.6 ≈ 2.0
Efficacy & Power
Luminous Efficacy
η = Φ ÷ P
η = efficacy (lm/W) · Φ = total luminous flux (lm) · P = input power (W). Use system watts (fixture + driver), not bare LED chip watts, for accurate real-world efficacy.
Example: 4500 lm output at 30 W system power → η = 4500 ÷ 30 = 150 lm/W
Annual Energy Cost
Cost = (P × H × tariff) ÷ 1000
P = system watts · H = annual burning hours · tariff = cost per kWh. Useful for ROI calculations when replacing legacy sources with LED.
Example: 30 W fixture × 4000 h/year × €0.25/kWh ÷ 1000 = €3.00/year per fixture
Inverse Square Law
Point source illuminance
E = I ÷ d²
E = illuminance (lx) · I = luminous intensity in that direction (cd) · d = distance from source to surface (m). Doubling the distance reduces illuminance to one quarter. Valid for point sources and narrow-beam downlights.
Example: Downlight with 2000 cd at 2 m → E = 2000 ÷ 4 = 500 lx. At 4 m → E = 2000 ÷ 16 = 125 lx
Chapter 07

Drivers & Controls

Every LED needs a driver — the power conversion and regulation unit sitting between mains supply and the LED junction. Understanding driver topology and control protocols is essential for specifying, commissioning, and troubleshooting real installations.

What is a Driver?

A driver converts mains AC (230 V) into a regulated DC output matched to the LED string's electrical requirements. It provides over-voltage, over-current, and short-circuit protection — without it, an LED would instantly burn out on a direct mains connection.

Constant Current (CC)

The most common LED driver type. Output current is fixed — typically 350 mA, 500 mA, 700 mA, or 1050 mA — while output voltage varies to maintain that current. Drive current directly determines lumen output and junction temperature, which in turn governs lumen maintenance life.

Constant Voltage (CV)

Supplies a fixed DC rail — most commonly 12 V or 24 V — while current varies with the connected load. LED strip lighting and some retrofit modules use this topology. The strip itself contains current-limiting resistors per segment. Less precise for colour consistency than CC but simpler to wire in long runs.

DALI Dimming

Digital Addressable Lighting Interface (IEC 62386). A two-wire digital bus where each gear has a unique short address (0–63), supporting 16 groups and 16 scene presets. Two-way communication allows the controller to read back actual dimming level, lamp failure status, and emergency test results. The professional standard for offices, retail, and public buildings.

0–10 V Dimming

An analogue control signal: 10 V = full output, 1 V = minimum dim (typically 10%), 0 V = off (via separate switched live). Simple, cheap, and widely compatible — common in commercial troffer retrofits and warehouses. No addressing, no feedback, no fault reporting. One control wire per zone.

Emergency Drivers

Self-contained battery-backed drivers maintain illumination during mains failure, as required by EN 1838 and IEC 60598-2-22. Available as self-contained (battery in luminaire), central battery, or maintained/non-maintained variants. DALI-emergency (IEC 62386 Part 202) enables automated test scheduling and pass/fail reporting — eliminating manual monthly tests across large sites.

Chapter 08

Lighting Design Principles

Good lighting design is invisible — the light does its job without calling attention to itself. These principles form the thinking behind how lighting professionals translate a brief into a specification.

The Three Layers

Every well-designed space uses ambient light (general illumination for safe movement), task light (directed, higher-lux illumination at the work plane), and accent light (decorative, focal, or architectural emphasis). Relying on a single layer produces flat, institutional-feeling environments. Layering gives a space depth, hierarchy, and adaptability.

Uniformity (U₀)

Uniformity ratio U₀ = minimum lux ÷ average lux across the task area. EN 12464-1 requires U₀ ≥ 0.40 for most workspaces and ≥ 0.60 for corridors and circulation routes. Poor uniformity — bright pools under luminaires with dark gaps between — causes visual fatigue as the eye continuously re-adapts.

Avoiding Shadows

Shadows at the task plane are caused by the worker's own body blocking overhead luminaires. Solutions include lateral or indirect luminaire positioning, a 1.5× spacing-to-mounting-height ratio for good horizontal spread, and uplight or diffuse ambient components that fill shadow areas from multiple angles.

Mounting Height Effect

By the inverse square law, doubling mounting height reduces workplane illuminance to one quarter. A fixture delivering 500 lx at 2 m delivers only 125 lx at 4 m. Beam angle selection must compensate — narrow beams are needed at height to maintain centre-beam intensity, while wider beams suit lower mounting positions with better uniformity.

Colour Consistency

Even LEDs at the same nominal CCT vary in actual colour — measured in SDCM (MacAdam ellipses). SDCM ≤ 3 is imperceptible in most applications; SDCM ≤ 2 is specified for retail and healthcare where side-by-side comparison is critical. Mixed batches of SDCM 5+ units are visibly inconsistent on linear runs and ceilings.

Specification Thinking

A lux calculation is a starting point, not a final answer. Real specification moves from brief (task, user, standards) → photometric tool layout → maintained illuminance check (applying UF and MF) → glare assessment (UGR) → energy compliance (W/m²). On site, a commissioning luxmeter measurement confirms the design held up against real installation tolerances.
